I've been putting this off.

Not the migration — that's done. I mean writing about it. Because every "I moved my site from X to Y" post on the internet follows the same arc: *WordPress was slow, I found a new framework, here are my Lighthouse scores.*

I'll try not to do that to you.

---

WordPress and I had a decent run. It did what it was supposed to do — kept my site alive, let me post when I felt like it, handled the plumbing. I had zero complaints that felt urgent enough to act on.

That's actually the problem with WordPress for a site like mine. It doesn't break dramatically. It just quietly accumulates weight.

There was the plugin that handled SEO. The one that handled image optimization. The one that handled caching. The one that was supposed to make the caching plugin work better. At some point I had six plugins doing what I vaguely understood, and one of them was definitely fighting with another one — I just didn't know which.

The site worked. It was just... carrying a lot.

---

The moment that tipped me was loading my own homepage on mobile and watching the spinner go. Not for long. A second, maybe two. But enough to notice. Enough to feel like the site was thinking about showing up rather than just showing up.

I work on web performance for a living. I spend my days thinking about Core Web Vitals and TTFB and crawlability. And my personal website — the one with my name on it — was sitting on a shared WordPress host, loading fonts the slow way, building pages from a database on every single request.

That felt like a plumber with a leaky tap.

---

I'd been circling Astro for a while. The pitch is simple enough: ship almost zero JavaScript by default, generate everything at build time, and let the HTML do the work.

Which is honestly just... how the web was supposed to work.

The migration wasn't dramatic. I'm not going to pretend I rewrote everything from scratch in one heroic weekend. I moved the blog posts into `.mdx` files, set up content collections for the blog and photos, added proper JSON-LD schema (the kind I'd actually recommend to anyone else at work), and pointed the deploy at Cloudflare Pages.

The hardest part was the `<Image />` component. Not hard — just the thing I kept procrastinating on.

---

What surprised me wasn't the speed. I expected that. What surprised me was how much lighter the whole thing felt to *edit*.

With WordPress, adding a post meant logging into an admin panel, dealing with the block editor, remembering which plugin controlled which thing, and hoping the preview looked like the live version. With Astro, I open a `.mdx` file, write, push. That's it. No database. No admin UI. No login page sitting there waiting to be forgotten.

The site is just files now. Clean files with known contents. The kind of system that doesn't require remembering how it works.

---

There's a version of this post where I show you the before/after Lighthouse numbers. They're good. But that's not really the point.

The point is that the site now matches how I think about it in my head. Lightweight. Fast by default. No moving parts I can't explain.

I spent a lot of energy last year building systems at work that are clean, documented, and easy for someone else to pick up. It felt slightly inconsistent to come home to a personal site that was none of those things.

Now it isn't.

---

WordPress is fine. If you're running a content team, or you need a CMS your non-technical colleagues can use, or you just want to get something live without touching a terminal — use it. It earns its place.

But if your site is just *you* — your writing, your photos, a CV, a contact link — you probably don't need a database. You probably don't need six plugins maintaining a fragile truce with each other.

You might just need some files and a build step.
